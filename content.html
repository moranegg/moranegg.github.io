Problem solving with care and energy - turning passion into impact.

My name is Morane Ottilia Gruenpeter.

As a lifelong optimist, I believe there is always hope - even in the most uncertain situations. Change is inevitable, and we can choose where we to focus our effort and energy. Curiosity without limits if a gift I choose to offer myself. Asking questions and challenging the status quo are essential parts of my journey.

After several years as a professional harpist, I made the bold decision to change career paths and embark on a new journey in software engineering and emerging technologies. Today, as a passionate advocate for Open Science and Open Source Software, I’m committed to ensuring that software is recognized, preserved, and valued as a key part of our collective knowledge—across research, culture, and society. Whether through its archival, citation, or integration into global infrastructures, I work to support software as a durable and essential form of human expression and understanding.
My path has taken unexpected turns, and even surprised me at times. Growing up in Israel in a family that had to rebuild their lives from profound loss taught me to appreciate the simplest things.
As an established musician in Israel and after the creation of the IHS - Israeli Harpists Society, an association for harpists who wish to open new harp classes in Israeli conservatories, I've decided that I need to broden my perspective and continue music studies in Paris, the capital of the harp. This is why I have moved to France in 2011, thinking it's temporary.
Shortly after my arrival, a growing desire for adventure led me to pursue a new direction. Exploration of different activities, brought me to discover software coding. I remember to this day, the feeling I had while writing my first computer program in class - finding a solution to a problem through technology is an amazing feeling.
In 2017, during my Master's degree, I began an internship at. Inria with the Software Heritage team to "build the semantic web of Free and Open Source Software". This was big challenge to solve, and I've created this plan .
I remember how amazed I was by this idea to save source code and preserve it and at the same time build a massive collection of software with the full development history. Software Heritage is the universal archive of source code. The word universal matters—it means the archive is for everyone. Whether or not you’re a software engineer or have a background in computer science, this resource is meant to be open and accessible to all.
I first encountered Open Science when joining the FORCE11 Software Citation Implementation Working Group and the RDA Software Source Code Interest Group. These communities introduced me to the importance of transparency, reuse, and recognition in research—especially when it comes to software, which has long been underrepresented and underrecognized in academia. In that moment, I took on a personal mission—well beyond the scope of my day-to-day work—to dedicate effort and energy toward making a cultural change. Making software a first class research output. The journey is long, but seeing the evolution of this change is extremely satisfying. It requires patience, hope, and a lot of effort to sustain it and keep moving forward. Moreover, this change is a community-driven change and requires collaboration and connections.
On a personal note, I now live in France with my partner and our two playful daughters, continuously exploring new ways to appreciate life.
This is my story- shaped by curiosity and a passion for making a difference.
